How it works

What every number on Session and Progress means, and where it comes from. Two minutes to read.

From a call to numbers

Four steps, and only one of them uses AI.

  1. Record. The call is recorded in two separate audio channels — your voice and your partner's. So "who said what" is exact, never guessed.
  2. Transcribe. AssemblyAI turns the audio into a text transcript.
  3. Mark errors. An AI pass reads the transcript piece by piece and marks clear grammar errors: the exact phrase, a correction, and one category from the fixed list below. This is the only AI step.
  4. Count. Every number you see is plain arithmetic over those marks and the transcript: counting words, counting marks, dividing one by the other. No AI opinions, no scores invented by a model.

The seven error categories

Every marked error gets exactly one of these. The list is fixed so sessions stay comparable over time.

CategoryWhat it meansExample
Verb TenseWrong time form of the verbYesterday I go there → Yesterday I went there
Verb FormAgreement, to/-ing, modals, participlesHe don't know → He doesn't know
ArticlesMissing, extra or wrong a/an/theI have car → I have a car
PrepositionsWrong or missing prepositionIt depends of him → It depends on him
Word OrderWrong order, broken sentence structureI know what should I do → I know what I should do
Wrong WordWrong, extra or non-existent wordsomething like else → something else
CollocationUnnatural word pairing, literal translationmake a photo → take a photo

Reading an evidence card

Example: "Wrong Word — 9 cases · 26% of errors · 1.03/100w · Seen in 3 of last 3 comparable sessions · density 1.34 → 1.58 → 0.88 → 1.03".

9 cases — you made 9 marked errors of this category in this session.

26% of errors — the share among all your marked errors that session.

1.03/100w — density: errors ÷ your English words × 100. This is the main number. It is fair to compare between a short call and a long one, because it is "errors per 100 words", not a raw count.

Seen in 3 of last 3 comparable sessions — the category keeps coming back. A session is comparable when you spoke at least 120 English words in it; the category counts as "seen" when its density there was at least 0.3/100w.

density 1.34 → 1.58 → 0.88 → 1.03 — the density in the three previous sessions, then in this one. Falling numbers = the pattern is closing.

Card order — categories that keep repeating come first, then the most frequent ones. Nothing else affects the order.

This session's numbers

The panel at the bottom of the Session page. All computed directly from the transcript.

NumberWhat it is
English wordsHow many words you said in English. Russian words are not counted here.
Words / turnAverage length of your speaking turn. Longer turns usually mean more developed answers instead of one-liners.
Speaking shareYour part of all words in the call, in percent. Around 50% means a balanced conversation.
Error densityAll your marked errors per 100 English words. Example: 34 errors in 877 words → 34 ÷ 877 × 100 = 3.88.
Fillersum, uh, you know, I mean, kind of… per 100 words. Lower = cleaner speech.
Russian fallbackPercent of your words said in Russian — switching to Russian when the English word did not come.
Lexical diversityMATTR, from 0 to 1: how varied your vocabulary is. Higher = you repeat the same words less.

The focus loop

The whole point of the tool: pick a weakness, work on it, close it.

  1. On the Session page, press Add to focus on a card worth working on (up to 3 at a time). The card's density on that day becomes the baseline.
  2. Speak on the next calls with that pattern in mind.
  3. The Focus of the week block compares the baseline with the session you are viewing: "0.86 → 0.46/100w (−47%)". A drop of 40% or more, with at least 120 English words spoken, shows ✓ ready to close.
  4. Press Close — the focus moves to Closed focuses on the Progress page. That list is the victory log.

The system only proposes; closing is always your decision.

How much to trust the numbers

Exact: who said what, word counts, speaking share, fillers, Russian fallback, vocabulary variety — these are computed mechanically and are fully reproducible.

Approximate: error marks come from AI, so two runs on the same transcript can differ by a few findings. Single errors mean little; repeated categories and multi-session trends are what matters.

Low sample: sessions with under 120 English words are shown but not used for comparisons, and Progress draws them as hollow points.

Sessions annotated before July 2026 used an older, noisier extraction. "Re-run annotations" on a session refreshes it with the current one.
