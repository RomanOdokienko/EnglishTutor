How it works

What every number on Session and Progress means, and where it comes from. Two minutes to read.

From a call to numbers

Four steps, and only one of them uses AI.

  1. Record. The call is recorded in two separate audio channels — your voice and your partner's. So "who said what" is exact, never guessed.
  2. Transcribe. AssemblyAI turns the audio into a text transcript.
  3. Mark errors. An AI pass reads the transcript piece by piece and marks clear grammar errors: the exact phrase, a correction, and one category from the fixed list below. This is the only AI step.
  4. Count. Every number you see is plain arithmetic over those marks and the transcript: counting words, counting marks, dividing one by the other. No AI opinions, no scores invented by a model.

The seven error categories

Every marked error gets exactly one of these. The list is fixed so sessions stay comparable over time.

CategoryWhat it meansExample
Verb TenseWrong time form of the verbYesterday I go there → Yesterday I went there
Verb FormAgreement, to/-ing, modals, participlesHe don't know → He doesn't know
ArticlesMissing, extra or wrong a/an/theI have car → I have a car
PrepositionsWrong or missing prepositionIt depends of him → It depends on him
Word OrderWrong order, broken sentence structureI know what should I do → I know what I should do
Wrong WordWrong, extra or non-existent wordsomething like else → something else
CollocationUnnatural word pairing, literal translationmake a photo → take a photo

How serious an error is (severity)

Since July 2026 every marked error also carries a severity level — how much it hurts the listener, judged for that exact phrase, not for the category.

LevelWhat it meansExample
blockingThe meaning is distorted or the listener must strain to reconstruct itfive minutes later before our meeting started → five minutes before our meeting started
noticeableA clear error the listener registers, but the meaning survivesI'm not understand → I don't understand
minorA small slip most listeners would not even noticebuilt early prototype → built an early prototype

Severity is independent of how often an error happens, and it is why a rare but gross error can no longer hide behind frequent small ones: Session and This week always show a Most serious block with the worst level present, and Progress tracks Serious errors only (blocking + noticeable) as its own line. Blocking is legitimately rare at your level — most weeks the worst you will see is noticeable, and that is good news. Severity never changes the error counts themselves.

Fluency from the recording

Andrey's request: grammar is one axis, flow is another. These numbers exist only for recorded calls — a pasted text transcript carries no timing.

NumberWhat it is
Speech rateWords per minute of your own speaking time. Comfortable conversational English is roughly 110–150 wpm; the trend matters more than the absolute number.
Hesitation pauses / minSilences of half a second or longer inside your own utterances, per speaking minute. Pauses while the other person talks are not counted.
Words between pausesHow long you speak without hesitating, on average. Longer runs = smoother delivery.

All of this is plain arithmetic over word timestamps from the transcription — no AI judgement involved, fully reproducible. Where these numbers are absent (text uploads, old sessions), they are simply not measured; absence never counts as zero. The 0.5s pause threshold is a v1 working hypothesis, to be recalibrated on real recorded calls.

Reading your personal session review

Session compares each participant only with up to three of their own comparable calls before the selected date.

Previous-call average — the average error density from up to three calls before the selected session, each with at least 120 English words, completed annotations and matching metric versions. The exact reference dates are shown in Session.

Findings — every raw annotation of that category counted in this session. Open a category row to inspect the exact phrase, correction and explanation, or jump to it in the transcript.

Rate — errors ÷ your English words × 100. This is the fair comparison between calls of different lengths.

Share — the category's share among all your mapped errors in this session.

Vs previous calls — the absolute difference from that previous-call average. Small changes within 10% (or 0.15/100w) are shown as steady instead of being treated as progress or regression.

Top three priorities — categories with the largest current rate rise when they are also above your recent average or recurring. An active focus gets a small tie-breaker. These three categories become the compact practice plan.

Practice examples — a smaller quality-filtered subset of the raw findings. These are used for exercises; they do not change the category total.

This is an “as of that day” comparison: future calls never rewrite an older session. Re-analysing, deleting or adding an earlier call can change later comparisons because their source evidence changed. A session below 120 English words remains available for transcript review but is not scored.

Reading This week and Progress

This week — one focus, two patterns, two phrases to rehearse and the most serious findings of the latest call, per person. Patterns and the three displayed values come from the latest three comparable calls. Its status still compares the newest call with the three calls before it.

Progress summary — the latest comparable value versus the average of the previous three comparable calls. The full chart keeps every comparable historical point; it does not use the first-ever value as the summary baseline.

Partner challenge — both people stay visible together on Progress, but every status is calculated against that person's own history. One person's error rate never becomes the other's baseline.

This session's numbers

The panel at the bottom of the Session page. All computed directly from the transcript.

NumberWhat it is
English wordsHow many words you said in English. Russian words are not counted here.
Words / turnAverage length of your speaking turn. Longer turns usually mean more developed answers instead of one-liners.
Speaking shareYour part of all words in the call, in percent. Around 50% means a balanced conversation.
Error densityAll your marked errors per 100 English words. Example: 34 errors in 877 words → 34 ÷ 877 × 100 = 3.88.
Fillersum, uh, you know, I mean, kind of… per 100 words. Lower = cleaner speech.
Lexical diversityMATTR, from 0 to 1: how varied your vocabulary is. Higher = you repeat the same words less.

The focus loop

The whole point of the tool: pick a weakness, work on it, close it.

  1. On the Session page, open a practice priority and press Add to focus (up to 3 at a time). The category's density on that day becomes the baseline.
  2. Speak on the next calls with that pattern in mind.
  3. The Focus of the week block compares the baseline with the session you are viewing: "0.86 → 0.46/100w (−47%)". A drop of 40% or more, with at least 120 English words spoken, shows ✓ ready to close.
  4. Press Close — the focus moves to Closed focuses on the Progress page. That list is the victory log.

The system only proposes; closing is always your decision.

How much to trust the numbers

Exact: who said what, word counts, speaking share, fillers and vocabulary variety — these are computed mechanically and are fully reproducible.

Approximate: error marks come from AI, so two runs on the same transcript can differ by a few findings. Single errors mean little; repeated categories and multi-session trends are what matters.

Low sample: sessions with under 120 English words are shown but not used for comparisons, and Progress draws them as hollow points.

In July 2026 the whole archive was re-annotated with the current, measured extraction (gpt-5-mini, two passes; ~82% precision on a hand-labeled set). Densities stepped up across the board compared to the older model — that is the measurement getting more complete, not your English getting worse. The series is uniform again: every stored session now uses the same model, prompt and severity rules, recorded per session in its analysis version.
